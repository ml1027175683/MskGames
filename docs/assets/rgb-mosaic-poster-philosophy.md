# Chromatic Extraction

Chromatic Extraction treats color as a mined substance: not decoration, but material pulled from a dark technical earth. Space should feel like a precision instrument, with broad fields of black and mineral shadow interrupted by controlled RGB eruptions. Form emerges through grids, strata, and measured fragments, as if the image were both a mine map and a pixel canvas under laboratory observation.

The visual language is dense but disciplined. Repeated pixel units, numbered traces, spectral seams, and hard-edged geometry should communicate a system at work without explaining it in paragraphs. The final piece must look meticulously crafted, the product of deep expertise and countless small decisions, where every alignment, glow, and edge has been labored over with care.

Color behaves like inventory. Red, green, and blue appear as precious deposits, isolated samples, or signals crossing the composition. Neutral tones carry weight: carbon black, oxidized brown, muted parchment, and faint steel. The palette should never feel random; it should feel painstakingly calibrated by someone at the top of their field, with each chromatic accent placed because the whole composition requires it.

Typography is sparse and functional, more like machine labeling than advertising copy. Words are allowed only as anchors, coordinates, or quiet declarations embedded into the structure. The image should be understood first through spatial rhythm, contrast, and accumulated marks. Text is not the message; it is a small instrument panel attached to a much larger visual engine.

The composition should balance game energy with museum-level restraint. It may feel industrial, digital, and slightly ominous, but never cartoonish. Craftsmanship is the primary signal: crisp grids, controlled noise, exact margins, and master-level execution. The viewer should feel that the artifact took countless hours to refine, even when the visible language remains minimal.
